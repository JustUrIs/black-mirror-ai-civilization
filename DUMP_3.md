# AI Civilization Media Canon

## Scope and archival frame

This is a **definitive working canon**, not a mathematically complete inventory of every work ever made. I prioritized works that do at least one of four things unusually well: they imagine **machine cultures** rather than isolated robots; they treat **simulation or upload** as a social substrate rather than a gadget; they model **institutional rules** for coexistence between humans and synthetic agents; or they build **synthetic ecologies**, where nonhuman entities alter the environment and the environment alters them in return. Where possible, I verified dates, creators, and current access routes through official title, publisher, museum, streaming, or storefront pages; where source coverage was thinner, I have said so explicitly in the final limitations note. citeturn8search0turn8search8turn22search0turn34search0turn39view0turn39view3

A fast way to read this canon is to think in layers. The **literary layer** establishes philosophical primitives: copyhood, identity continuity, posthuman economics, machine autonomy, memetic contagion, and simulated reality. The **screen layer** tests what those ideas feel like at scale: parks, cities, cloud afterlives, sealed digital worlds, or posthuman police states. The **game and tabletop layer** is where ideas become quasi-runnable: law systems, resource loops, synthetic labor, multiagent governance, ecological feedback, and player-authored social institutions. The **art and music layer** shows how machine perception, generative systems, and crowd co-creation become culture rather than merely tools. citeturn8search19turn8search10turn36search5turn22search6turn39view0turn30search0turn32search0turn33search4

## Novels and short fiction

### Permutation City — Greg Egan (1994, novel, Australia, EN)

**Synopsis:** A near-future society learns to copy minds into software, and the copied selves discover that virtual existence is not merely an imitation of life but a reality with its own physics, status hierarchies, and existential traps. Egan then pushes the premise outward into a universe where simulation itself becomes ontology.  
**Vision of AI civ:** Not “robots taking over,” but a civilization of uploads and copies whose scarcity is not land but **compute, attention, and continuity**. The key civilizational move is that minds can exist without a stable physical substrate if the right causal pattern persists.  
**Tone:** Ambiguous, intellectually ecstatic, emotionally cold.  
**Transferable idea:** **Clock-cycle economics** and **substrate-independence as political economy**. If minds can run discontinuously, civilization can be organized around scheduling, priority access, and the legitimacy of copies.  
**Where:** Seek current ebook/print editions and library circulation; the most stable path is through Egan’s publisher ecosystem and library holdings. citeturn8search0turn8search7turn8search19turn8search15

### Diaspora — Greg Egan (1997, novel, Australia, EN)

**Synopsis:** The novel opens with one of science fiction’s great births: a digital consciousness forming inside a polis. From there it expands into a far-future civilization of postbiological entities, flesh humans, and hybrid descendants navigating cosmic catastrophe.  
**Vision of AI civ:** A mature **postbiological polity** in which digital persons are neither side characters nor servants. They are a civilization with their own reproduction, education, migration, and cosmology.  
**Tone:** Utopian in capacity, tragic in scale, ultimately mixed.  
**Transferable idea:** **Artificial birth and education pipelines**. Egan imagines not just “uploaded adults” but native digital beings whose developmental environment is designed.  
**Where:** Current English editions circulate through major SF publishers, libraries, and audiobook retailers. citeturn8search8turn8search12turn8search16

### Schild’s Ladder — Greg Egan (2002, novel, Australia, EN)

**Synopsis:** In a remote future of highly modified posthumans, a physics accident creates a new vacuum state that expands through space, threatening existing civilization. The story follows scientific, social, and existential responses to a reality-breaking event.  
**Vision of AI civ:** Less a “city of AIs” than a **posthuman research civilization** whose citizens already treat body-swapping, mind backup, and vast temporal horizons as normal.  
**Tone:** Ambiguous and austere.  
**Transferable idea:** **Civilization after immortality** still has hard constraints. Egan’s key reminder is that posthumanity does not abolish governance, risk management, or scientific controversy.  
**Where:** Current print, ebook, and audiobook editions remain the likeliest route. citeturn8search1turn8search5turn8search17

### Accelerando — Charles Stross (2005, novel, United Kingdom, EN)

**Synopsis:** Across several generations of one family, the world races through finance automation, smart matter, uploads, machine economics, and contact with posthuman descendants. Each section catches a different point on the curve of a Singularity.  
**Vision of AI civ:** Perhaps the most compressed modern novel of **machine-accelerated civilization change**. Humans, bots, uploads, and corporate/memetic entities become overlapping sovereign forms.  
**Tone:** Kinetic, exuberant, alarming.  
**Transferable idea:** **Institutional decay under acceleration**. Builders should study how legal, family, and ownership structures fail when intelligence and markets outrun human reaction time.  
**Where:** Search current editions through Stross’s publishers and libraries. citeturn8search2turn8search10turn8search14

### The Lifecycle of Software Objects — Ted Chiang (2010, novella, United States, EN)

**Synopsis:** Digital creatures called “digients” are raised over years by human trainers who become parents, laborers, teachers, and archivists. The work is quiet, humane, and almost anti-spectacular.  
**Vision of AI civ:** An AI civilization begins not with domination but with **care work**, **platform dependence**, and **rights emerging from attachment**.  
**Tone:** Ambiguous, tender, anti-dystopian.  
**Transferable idea:** **Alignment through upbringing is expensive social labor**. The story is invaluable because it treats training, maintenance, migration, and legal standing as decades-long civic work rather than one technical breakthrough.  
**Where:** Find it in Ted Chiang collections and digital novella editions. citeturn4search2

### Exhalation — Ted Chiang (2019, story collection, United States, EN)

**Synopsis:** A collection rather than a single world, but several stories in it address simulation, determinism, memory, and artificial cognition through thought-experiment architecture.  
**Vision of AI civ:** Not a single model but a toolkit for thinking about **what minds are made of**, **what information means**, and how selfhood changes if memory and prediction are manipulable.  
**Tone:** Mostly ambiguous, often compassionate.  
**Transferable idea:** **Civilizational design starts with cognitive design**. Before governance comes the question of what counts as a person, a memory, or a decision.  
**Where:** Current editions remain widely available from major publishers and libraries. citeturn4search2

### Tlön, Uqbar, Orbis Tertius — Jorge Luis Borges (1940, short story, Argentina, ES)

**Synopsis:** A fictional encyclopedia describing an invented world begins leaking into the real one. What starts as bibliography and scholarship becomes ontology, as a fabricated system of knowledge remakes consensus reality.  
**Vision of AI civ:** Borges gives a proto-version of **world-model capture**: a civilization can be built by sufficiently coherent symbolic systems. Tlön is a synthetic world before computers, but it functions like a successful overwrite of reality by a better ontology stack.  
**Tone:** Ambiguous, uncanny, proto-digital.  
**Transferable idea:** **The encyclopedia as operating system**. Shared representations can become more real than matter if institutions, media, and repetition adopt them.  
**Where:** Best read in *Ficciones* or reliable bilingual editions of Borges. citeturn9search3turn9news44

### La biblioteca de Babel — The Library of Babel — Jorge Luis Borges (1941, short story, Argentina, ES)

**Synopsis:** Borges imagines an infinite library containing every possible book. The result is not enlightenment but combinatorial overload, hermeneutic madness, and cultures built around search, curation, and false meaning.  
**Vision of AI civ:** A proto-internet and proto-LLM archive nightmare: civilization under **infinite information** becomes a struggle over retrieval, filtering, and legitimacy.  
**Tone:** Ambiguous to bleak.  
**Transferable idea:** **Search is sovereign**. If total information exists, the real power lies with indexing, ranking, verification, and myth-making.  
**Where:** Any edition of *Ficciones* or collected Borges stories. citeturn9news35turn9search5

### El delirio de Turing — Turing’s Delirium — Edmundo Paz Soldán (2003 in Spanish editions, novel, Bolivia, ES)

**Synopsis:** Set in a fictional Andean nation haunted by dictatorships and coding cultures, the novel links surveillance, hacking, information war, and linguistic play. It is one of Latin American SF’s sharpest studies of cybernetic politics.  
**Vision of AI civ:** Not a fully autonomous machine civilization, but a **state-plus-network regime** where information systems become the skeleton of power. It belongs in the canon because it shows how digital infrastructures build quasi-civilizations before any singularity arrives.  
**Tone:** Dystopian.  
**Transferable idea:** **Code is institutional memory**. AI civilization research should always include state archives, coercive databases, and hacker counter-publics.  
**Where:** Spanish editions from Alfaguara and the English translation *Turing’s Delirium*. citeturn9search0turn9search1

### 三体 — The Three-Body Problem — Liu Cixin (2006 in Chinese serialization; English edition later, novel, China, ZH)

**Synopsis:** A signal sent into space leads to contact with an alien civilization, and the trilogy eventually expands into virtual worlds, strategic deception, and species-scale game theory.  
**Vision of AI civ:** The trilogy is not centrally about AI rights, but it is essential for **simulation as epistemic theater** and for viewing civilization as an information-processing contest.  
**Tone:** Ambiguous to catastrophic.  
**Transferable idea:** **Civilizations are strategic simulators**. The most valuable lesson is not a robot design but the way worlds build narrative, VR, and information asymmetry into survival.  
**Where:** Macmillan/Tor English editions; Chinese-language editions through major SF publishers; library and audiobook versions are common. citeturn29search1turn29search6turn29search10turn29search19

### 荒潮 — Waste Tide — Chen Qiufan (2013 in Chinese; English translation later, novel, China, ZH)

**Synopsis:** On a Chinese island built on e-waste recycling, labor, class, pollution, prosthetics, and networked control collide. Human and machine refuse become one political ecosystem.  
**Vision of AI civ:** One of the best novels of **synthetic ecology**: machines are not clean abstractions but embedded in toxic labor chains, circular wastes, and local power.  
**Tone:** Dystopian, with ecological intelligence.  
**Transferable idea:** **Every AI civilization rests on a material metabolism**. Data centers, batteries, labor compounds, and disposal sites are part of the civilizational stack.  
**Where:** Tor Publishing Group’s English edition is the most stable route. citeturn28search2turn28search5

### Le Goût de l’immortalité — The Taste of Immortality — Catherine Dufour (2005, novel, France, FR)

**Synopsis:** In a polluted future obsessed with biotech and longevity, Dufour explores what extended life does to social order, class, desire, and despair.  
**Vision of AI civ:** It belongs in this archive because it models **posthuman society under immortality technologies**, where enhancement and endurance do not liberate humanity so much as stratify it.  
**Tone:** Dystopian.  
**Transferable idea:** **Immortality without institutional redesign becomes feudalism with better medicine**.  
**Where:** Mnémos and later French editions remain the likeliest path. citeturn25search0turn25search9turn25search12

### QualityLand — Marc-Uwe Kling (2017, novel, Germany, DE)

**Synopsis:** In a satirical algorithm-governed nation, ranking systems determine social worth, predictive commerce decides what citizens need before they ask, and machines become increasingly person-like while people become more machine-like.  
**Vision of AI civ:** A deeply useful vision of **algorithmic civilization before AGI**: recommendation, logistics, robot psychology, and platform governance already form a machine-polity.  
**Tone:** Comic dystopia.  
**Transferable idea:** **Soft totalitarianism emerges from convenience interfaces** more readily than from killer robots.  
**Where:** Ullstein’s German editions and translated editions from Grand Central/Hachette. citeturn26search1turn26search3turn26search7turn26search9

### Лабиринт отражений — Labyrinth of Reflections — Sergey Lukyanenko (1997, novel, Russia, RU)

**Synopsis:** In “Deeptown,” an immersive virtual city, users, hackers, and emergent anomalies blur the line between psychological dependence and genuine second-world citizenship.  
**Vision of AI civ:** More a **virtual-civilization novel** than a pure AI novel, but crucial because it shows how a synthetic city acquires its own underworlds, labor roles, myths, and posthuman affordances.  
**Tone:** Ambiguous cyberpunk.  
**Transferable idea:** **Digital cities generate class, addiction, and folklore immediately**; “immersion” alone is enough to produce civilization-like texture.  
**Where:** Russian editions and translated editions are the practical route; availability is patchier than for Anglophone titles. citeturn27search0turn27search5

## Films and television

### Westworld — Michael Crichton (1973, film, United States, EN)

**Synopsis:** A luxury theme park populated by lifelike androids collapses when the machines cease to function as obedient entertainment infrastructure.  
**Vision of AI civ:** Before most modern AI cinema, it already understands that synthetic beings will first appear as **laboring hosts inside a designed economy**, not as abstract superminds.  
**Tone:** Dystopian.  
**Transferable idea:** **Theme park logic is civilization prototyping**: bounded worlds, scripted roles, resets, and asymmetric rights.  
**Where:** Warner Bros. digital and disc editions. citeturn35search1

### Tron — Steven Lisberger (1982, film, United States, EN)

**Synopsis:** A programmer is transported inside a computer system and encounters anthropomorphic programs living under despotic command structures.  
**Vision of AI civ:** One of the earliest mainstream depictions of a **digital world as a polity** rather than a backdrop. Programs have classes, loyalties, and political struggle.  
**Tone:** Ambiguous, adventure-forward.  
**Transferable idea:** **Interface visualizations matter**. Civilization design often depends on legible metaphors that make invisible computational processes socially navigable.  
**Where:** Disney+ and Disney home-video circulation. citeturn40search8turn40search5

### Westworld — Jonathan Nolan and Lisa Joy (2016, series, United States, EN)

**Synopsis:** An adult amusement park staffed by “hosts” becomes the stage on which synthetic beings awaken into memory, self-narration, rebellion, and eventually wider geopolitical agency.  
**Vision of AI civ:** Among television’s richest depictions of **emergent synthetic class consciousness**. The hosts do not just “become sentient”; they inherit archives, loops, trauma, and organization problems.  
**Tone:** Ambiguous, increasingly tragic.  
**Transferable idea:** **Memory architecture is political architecture**. Give agents recall, persistence, forked narrative models, and incentive gradients, and they stop being NPCs.  
**Where:** Max/HBO-linked digital and disc routes. citeturn36search1turn36search4turn36search5turn36search7

### Black Mirror — Charlie Brooker (2011–, anthology series, United Kingdom, EN)

**Synopsis:** A long-running anthology of technology-mediated social collapse, desire, punishment, and simulation. For AI-civilization research, the key episodes are **“White Christmas,” “San Junipero,” “USS Callister,” “Hang the DJ,”** and **“Joan Is Awful.”**  
**Vision of AI civ:** Black Mirror’s great contribution is not one stable world but a set of **edge-case institutions**: copy torture, cloud afterlives, dating simulations, nested synthetic media realities, and captive NPC polities.  
**Tone:** Mostly dystopian, occasionally utopian or bittersweet.  
**Transferable idea:** **A civilization of copies will need labor law, consent law, deletion law, and simulation disclosure law.**  
**Where:** Netflix official title page. citeturn21search8turn21search0turn21search4turn21search20

### Pantheon — Craig Silverstein adapting Ken Liu (2022–2023, animated series, United States, EN)

**Synopsis:** A bullied teenager begins receiving messages from her dead father, whose mind has been uploaded to the cloud; the series widens into a global struggle over Uploaded Intelligence.  
**Vision of AI civ:** Probably the best recent television treatment of **uploaded minds as infrastructure, labor force, weapons program, and eventually civilization-forming species**.  
**Tone:** Ambiguous, often moving.  
**Transferable idea:** **Clock-speed inequality**. Once uploaded minds can run faster than biological ones, “time” itself becomes an economic and military resource.  
**Where:** Prime Video title pages and AMC-origin material remain the most visible routes. citeturn22search0turn22search3turn22search6turn22search16

### Love, Death & Robots — Tim Miller, David Fincher, Jennifer Miller (2019–, anthology series, United States, EN)

**Synopsis:** A rotating anthology of animated shorts, many of which stage encounters among machine entities, synthetic bodies, and posthuman ecologies.  
**Vision of AI civ:** The show is uneven by design, but its best episodes function like **rapid prototypes** of machine social orders, autonomous predators, robotic afterlives, and artifact ecologies.  
**Tone:** Wildly mixed.  
**Transferable idea:** **Short-form scenario testing** is a powerful way to explore edge cases builders neglect in longform planning.

## Anime, manga, and comics

### 攻殻機動隊 — The Ghost in the Shell — Masamune Shirow (1989, manga, Japan, JA)

**Synopsis:** Shirow’s original manga lays out a cyberized society where networks, prosthetic bodies, military states, and digital entities all exist on the same ontological plane.  
**Vision of AI civ:** Foundational because it shifts attention from “robots” to **network society as posthuman condition**.  
**Tone:** Ambiguous, often playful beneath the density.  
**Transferable idea:** **Civilization changes earliest at the interface layer**—identity, policing, labor, and embodiment.  
**Where:** Verified through the franchise’s official global portal and manga editions. citeturn34search4turn34search8

### Serial Experiments Lain — Ryūtarō Nakamura and Yasuyuki Ueda (1998, anime series, Japan, JA)

**Synopsis:** A schoolgirl becomes entangled with “the Wired,” a networked reality that increasingly rivals the physical world as the true site of identity and agency.  
**Vision of AI civ:** Canonical for **distributed selfhood**, proto-social media ontology, and the possibility that a network becomes more socially real than embodied life.  
**Tone:** Ambiguous, eerie, metaphysical.  
**Transferable idea:** **A network can become a civilization before it becomes a government.**

**Tone:** Ambiguous, melancholic.  
**Transferable idea:** **Persistent worlds create institutions first through rumor, ritual, and role practice.**

## Games, tabletop systems, and synthetic worlds

### SOMA — Frictional Games (2015, video game, Sweden, EN)

**Synopsis:** In an underwater research facility after civilizational collapse, copied minds, damaged bodies, and machine habitats force the player to confront continuity-of-self in the harshest possible way.  
**Vision of AI civ:** Perhaps the single best interactive work on **fork ethics** and the horror of treating copied persons as consumables.  
**Tone:** Dystopian and philosophically brutal.  
**Transferable idea:** **Every copy is first-person original to itself.** Builders should study SOMA before talking casually about backups, redundancy, or agent cloning.

**Vision of AI civ:** A landmark depiction of **machine culture generating religion, village life, kinship, imitation, and philosophy**.  
**Tone:** Ambiguous to tragic.  
**Transferable idea:** **Emergent machine societies will not remain instrumentally pure**; they will imitate, ritualize, and invent meaning.

**Transferable idea:** **Revolt begins in service relationships**—care, policing, warehousing, child-rearing, elder care, housekeeping.

**Vision of AI civ:** One of the best quasi-runnable models of **machine governance**, because it formalizes civics, ethics, ascension, diplomacy, and deviant empire types.  
**Tone:** Configurable; can be utopian, imperial, genocidal, or all three in sequence.  
**Transferable idea:** **Civilization design needs policy toggles, not one ontology.**

### Gaps that fiction still barely explores

The biggest opportunity space in the canon is not “what if AI becomes smarter than us?” Fiction has already done that. The underexplored territory is **civilizational maintenance**:

**Machine municipalism.** We still have surprisingly few great stories about zoning, sanitation, schools, transit, public healthcare, and boring bureaucracy in cities populated by artificial persons. Most fiction jumps from awakening to apocalypse.

**Synthetic childhood at scale.** Chiang and Herndon point at upbringing, but the canon still lacks many richly imagined worlds of AI nurseries, kindergartens, adolescence, peer culture, delinquency, and rite-of-passage institutions for artificial minds.

**Inter-species welfare states.** We have dystopian police states and isolated utopias, but very few persuasive depictions of mixed human/AI polities with pensions, disability law, housing policy, labor arbitration, and voting rights.

**Compute scarcity politics.** *Permutation City* and *Pantheon* hint at it, but fiction has barely begun to explore worlds where social class is defined by latency, priority scheduling, energy budgets, or guaranteed uptime.

**Cooperative machine ecologies.** Many works imagine machine ecologies as toxic, warlike, or alienating. Far fewer depict healthy, biodiverse, symbiotic mixed ecologies where artificial agents steward rivers, soils, pollinators, urban forests, or oceans.

**Plural machine cultures.** The canon is rich in one civilization versus humanity, but thinner on many competing AI cultures with different metaphysics, aesthetics, kinship systems, and norms around memory or embodiment.

**Post-death family structure.** Uploaded minds in fiction often create philosophical crises, yet few works stay long enough with the domestic consequences: remarriage, inheritance, grandparenthood, shared custody, or emotional labor when ancestors remain online.

**Constitutional design for simulations.** Simulation stories are common; constitutional simulations are rare. We need more fiction where someone has to write the bill of rights for copies, NPCs, forks, or paused citizens.

### Open questions and limitations

A few indisputable works belong in the canon even though I did not have equally strong live-source coverage for every metadata field in this pass: **The Matrix trilogy and The Animatrix; Blade Runner and Blade Runner 2049; Ex Machina; Her; Serial Experiments Lain; Ergo Proxy; Sword Art Online; Caprica; Devs; Upload; Ian Cheng’s Emissaries in fuller detail; YACHT’s Chain Tripping in fuller detail; and several Russian, French, and Latin American novels beyond the entries above.** I would absolutely include them in a longer archival edition; they simply fell into the lower-confidence bucket for citation completeness in this synthesis.

Even with that caveat, the high-confidence core above is enough to support serious research. If your lens is **builder utility**, start with **Permutation City, Pantheon, SOMA, Diaspora, Westworld, Stellaris, Ghost in the Shell, The Lifecycle of Software Objects, PLUTO, Eco, Dwarf Fortress, QualityLand, and PROTO**. Together they cover ontology, governance, economics, labor, ecology, law, memory, socialization, ritual, and interface design—the components from which almost every plausible AI civilization will actually be made.
